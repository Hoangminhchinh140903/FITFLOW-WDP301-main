2. Product Background
In recent years, Vietnam has witnessed a massive surge in sports and fitness activities, with a rapidly growing community of active individuals participating in sports such as badminton, pickleball, tennis, and running. According to recent market reports on Vietnam's fitness and sports industry, the demand for sports-related products and services continues to experience strong double-digit growth. The rising awareness of health and wellness, combined with the booming popularity of trend-driven sports like pickleball, has led to a significant increase in sports participation across major cities and local communities.
This strong growth in the sports sector has significantly increased the demand for sports apparel and gear rental and sales services. Alongside purchasing equipment, many beginners, casual players, and tourists prefer to rent high-quality gear (such as specialized rackets, footwear, and protective accessories) rather than making a large upfront investment. Furthermore, sports tournaments, corporate team-building events, and weekend sports clubs frequently require bulk rentals of equipment and uniform apparel.
Currently, numerous sports retailers and local court pro shops operate around busy sports complexes. These stores provide various sports apparel and gear with rental prices commonly ranging from 50,000 VND to 200,000 VND per session for equipment like rackets or specialized shoes. Many businesses also sell complementary accessories such as grip tapes, shuttlecocks, pickleballs, and sports drinks to enhance the customer experience.
During weekends, local tournaments, and peak evening hours, a single sports shop may process dozens or even hundreds of customer requests. A sports product may exist in multiple sizes, weights, grip sizes, and condition levels, making inventory tracking and rental management increasingly difficult when handled manually.
However, many sports gear rental and sales businesses still rely on manual or semi-manual operational methods. Product information, rental orders, customer records, deposits, collateral information (e.g., ID cards), and return schedules are commonly managed using paper notes, Excel spreadsheets, phone calls, and social media messages. These approaches become inefficient and difficult to control as the number of customers and transactions increases.
In many cases, staff may spend approximately 10–20 minutes processing a single rental order manually, including checking gear availability, recording customer information, preparing deposits, confirming collateral documents, checking equipment condition (e.g., racket string tension), and scheduling return dates. During busy periods, this manual process increases the risk of operational mistakes, delayed customer service, inventory conflicts, and loss of important business information.
Current business challenges in sports apparel and gear rental and sales shops include:
Manual operational processes: Shop owners and staff often manage rental orders, sales orders, deposits, collateral records, and return schedules manually, which is time-consuming and highly error-prone.
Inventory inconsistency: Gear is frequently tracked without a centralized real-time system, making it difficult to determine whether an item is available, reserved, being rented, undergoing maintenance (e.g., string replacement), damaged, or lost.
Complex rental workflows: Managing both rental and sales operations simultaneously becomes complicated without standardized workflows, especially when handling deposits, late returns, compensation fees for broken equipment, refunds, and gear condition tracking.
Limited customer experience: Customers often cannot conveniently browse available gear, check product availability, reserve equipment online, or understand rental policies before visiting the shop, leading to reduced customer satisfaction and potential business loss.
Limited business monitoring: Without centralized reporting and analytics, business owners face difficulties in tracking revenue, rental performance, popular gear, customer behavior, seasonal demand, and staff performance.
Staff coordination difficulties: Managing staff shifts, operational tasks, and rental responsibilities manually may cause scheduling conflicts and reduce operational efficiency during peak periods.
To address these operational problems, a dedicated management platform is required to support the business needs of sports apparel and gear stores.
FitFlow is proposed as a web-based sports apparel and gear sales and rental management platform designed specifically for this business domain. The platform aims to centralize business data, standardize operational workflows, improve inventory visibility, and enhance customer experience through digital transformation.
The system supports essential business operations including product management, rental order management, sales order management, deposit and collateral tracking, return processing, refund handling, compensation management for damaged or lost items, inventory condition monitoring, and staff shift management. In addition, the platform provides analytics and reporting features to help business owners monitor revenue, rental activities, customer behavior, and operational performance more effectively.
FitFlow also integrates AI-assisted recommendation features to help customers discover suitable sports gear based on their skill level, playing style, and event purposes, thereby improving customer engagement and personalized user experiences.
Unlike generic e-commerce platforms, FitFlow focuses specifically on solving the operational challenges of sports apparel and gear rental businesses. The platform emphasizes rental lifecycle management, inventory condition tracking, deposit management, late-return handling, compensation processing, staff operation support, and AI-assisted customer experiences.
By digitizing and standardizing these workflows, FitFlow helps reduce manual errors, shorten customer handling time, improve inventory transparency, enhance customer convenience, and support more efficient business operations for sports gear stores.


3. Existing Solutions
To better understand the current landscape of sports apparel and gear rental and sales services, several real-world business models have been analyzed. These represent different operational approaches within the market, ranging from pure retail shops to service-oriented court pro shops.
3.1 Local Independent Sports Shops
Many local sports shops located near badminton or pickleball courts offer a mix of sales and basic rental services. They provide customers with essential gear such as rackets, shoes, and apparel.
One of the key strengths of these local shops is their proximity to the end-users and immediate availability of products. Customers can easily drop by to rent a racket or buy shuttlecocks before a game.
However, these shops exhibit several limitations. They completely lack an advanced booking and scheduling system, requiring customers to walk in and hope the gear is available. Furthermore, there is no integrated mechanism for managing rental workflows, including deposit tracking, return scheduling, and condition monitoring. The absence of automation in operational processes limits the scalability and efficiency of the business.
3.2 Large-Scale Sports Retail Chains (e.g., VNB Sports, Decathlon)
Large-scale sports providers specialize heavily in the sale of sports apparel and equipment. These businesses offer an extensive inventory with thousands of products catering to almost every sport imaginable.
The primary advantage of these chains lies in their vast product range and professional retail management systems. Customers have access to a wide selection of gear and can choose from various brands and categories.
Despite these strengths, their platforms operate entirely as retail e-commerce systems. They generally do not support rental operations. There is no structured system for managing short-term rentals, deposit tracking, or equipment return workflows. Therefore, they cannot serve the specific segment of customers looking for temporary gear rentals for tournaments or trial purposes.
3.3 Sports Complex Pro Shops (Integrated Court Services)
These are service-focused shops located directly inside major sports complexes. They emphasize delivering a complete customer experience by combining court booking with immediate gear rental.
A notable strength of these pro shops is their strong focus on customer convenience. The business provides curated, well-maintained gear for players right when they need it, ensuring a smooth service process.
However, their systems are primarily designed to support court booking rather than comprehensive rental and sales management. Standalone online rental functionalities are limited, and the platform does not provide a structured system for tracking the lifecycle of rented equipment, managing collateral, or operational data analytics.
3.4 Comparative Analysis
A comparative analysis of the existing solutions highlights several common limitations across the market. While each business offers certain advantages in terms of product variety or immediate convenience, none of the analyzed systems provide a fully integrated solution that combines rental management, sales operations, customer interaction, and business analytics specifically tailored for sports gear.

3.5 Implications for System Development
The analysis of existing solutions indicates a significant gap in the market for a comprehensive and integrated system tailored to sports apparel and gear rental businesses. Current platforms are either retail-only or lack customer-centric rental features, resulting in inefficiencies and reduced service quality.
Therefore, the FitFlow system is proposed to address these limitations by providing a unified platform that integrates rental and sales functionalities, standardizes business workflows, and enhances customer interaction. By leveraging modern web technologies and data-driven approaches, FitFlow aims to improve operational efficiency, transparency, and scalability for sports stores.
4. Business Opportunity
The booming sports and fitness movement in Vietnam has increased the demand for convenient and professional sports apparel and gear rental services. However, existing manual processes limit the ability of shop owners to scale operations, manage inventory effectively, and provide a seamless customer experience.
FitFlow offers an opportunity to:
* Centralize sales and rental management on a single platform.
* Reduce operational errors through automation.
* Enable customers to browse, book, and reserve sports gear online.
* Align with digital transformation trends in retail and sports services.
By integrating modern web technologies and user-centered design, the proposed system provides a competitive advantage over traditional rental methods and generic e-commerce platforms.

The vision of FitFlow is to become a comprehensive and intelligent management platform for sports apparel and gear rental and sales businesses.


The platform aims to:

	6.2 Project Limitations

2. Management Approach
2.1 Project Process 


  

Figure 4. Project Process
The project employs a Progressive and Incremental development model (Iterative and incremental development), flexibly combining Agile thinking (product growth shadowing) and the academic, supervisory framework of a graduation project (Waterfall-based milestones).


Overall Process Flow
* Project Initiation → Planning → Design → Implementation (Iterations) → Testing → Closing


Key Sub-processes
a. Scope & Requirements Management:
The entire business requirements specification (Sales Business, Rental Flow, Pro/CCCD Booking Management, Overdue Billing, Sports Equipment Damage Compensation) is documented in detail in the SRS.


The feature scope will be "frozen" at the start of each iteration.


Requirements arising outside the scope during development (e.g., expanding modules to connect with venues, tournaments) must be assessed for impact and delayed in subsequent phases to avoid the risk of falling behind schedule (Scope Creep).


b. Change Management:
Minor Changes: UI/UX adjustments, product display information, input data validation rules -> Processed directly within the current Iteration.
Major Changes: Changes to the overdue penalty calculation formula, refund/paperwork processing flow, product lock logic based on time slots (Real-time Booking Slot) -> Requires team discussion, impact analysis (Impact Analysis) on time/energy resources, and approval from the Team Leader and Mentor.


c. Time Management:
Project progress is tightly synchronized with any home learning schedule.
The first task is broken down using a Work Breakdown Structure (WBS) and tracked weekly through visual management tools (such as Trello/Jira). Actual progress is reported and collected during regular Mentor meetings.


d. Risk Management:
The team proactively identifies specific FitFlow risks early on (e.g., product locking algorithms based on overlapping schedules - excessive bookings, processing payments for any equipment when booking) to develop mitigation methods and contingency plans.
2.2 Quality Management
The quality of the FitFlow system is ensured throughout by harmoniously combining routine room control activities (Prevention) and error resolution (Correction).


Quality Objectives:
Ensure the absolute accuracy of the Rental Business Flow: Ensure the status of sports equipment (Ready - Registered - Under Maintenance/Networked - Broken/Lost) is always synchronized in real time.


No errors in financial logic: Calculating standard order sales revenue, hourly/daily revenue, deposits, late payment penalties, and compensation deductions.


High stability: The system operates smoothly, with clear access control among the four groups (Customers, Store Employees, Store Owners, System).


Quality Assurance Activities
a. Server Room Error Prevention:
Clearly define and communicate complex business rules in the SRS documentation before coding begins.
Apply a unified standard coding system and naming conventions for both frontend and backend.
Separate dual inventory and billing processes into small, independent modules for easier testing and maintenance.


b. Peer Review:


Conduct periodic internal peer reviews for SRS documentation and database design documents (Database Design).
Cross-check (Code Review) critical core auxiliary logic segments: scheduling conflict checking algorithm, payment webhook integration logic, logic for automatically changing the status of restricted orders.


c. System Testing:


Unit Testing: Test core functions (progressive charge calculation function, compensation calculation function based on percentage damage to rackets/shoes).


Integration Testing: Test interoperability between subsystems (ordering -> subtracting inventory quantity -> creating a PO order transaction on the payment gateway).


System Testing: Run end-to-end tests using realistic scenarios (e.g., Customer places a Pickleball deposit online -> Employee hands over the equipment and keeps the ID card -> Customer responds after 2 hours and makes a muddy face -> Employee charges a penalty and retaliates).


User Acceptance Testing (UAT): Conduct real-world acceptance testing based on the perspective and experience of the sports store owner and the player to ensure the product meets the needs of any market.

III. Software Requirement Specification
1. Requirement Overview
1.1 Context Diagram 
The Fitflow – Hoi An Traditional Clothing Sales and Rental Management System is a web-based application that supports sales and rental of traditional clothing. It replaces largely manual and semi-manual store work—such as taking bookings, handling deposits, issuing and receiving rentals, and keeping track of stock—with a single coherent workflow inside one system boundary.
The context diagram (Level 0) defines that boundary for Release 1.0 and shows how Fitflow exchanges data with human actors and external systems. Guests may search the catalog, use the cart, register, leave contact details, complete guest checkout for sale orders, and receive product information, blog content, and registration confirmations. Customers extend this with accounts (registration and login), orders, feedback, and submission of body-measurement images; they receive product listings and invoices. Staff supply product status updates, rental confirmations, blog content, and walk-in orders, and receive booking outcomes (confirm/reject) and order assignments. Owners provide staff information, product configuration, promotion policy, and system settings, and participate in management loops for blogs (approve/reject/publish), vouchers, review moderation, staff shifts, and categories; they receive revenue reports, inventory statistics, and customer insights.
Outside the core web stack, Release 1.0 also integrates third-party services shown on the diagram. PayOS receives payment requests and returns transaction status for deposits and other payments. Google acts as an authentication provider: the system sends authentication requests and receives verified identity information. Cloudinary receives uploaded images/media and returns hosted URLs. Email carries notifications from the system (and, where applicable, delivery-related feedback). AIService (Groq and ChromaDB) receives queries and product-related context used for embeddings and retrieval, and returns AI responses and recommendation results. API4.ai exchanges processed data with the system—aligned with measurement-oriented or image-based processing flows (e.g. data derived from customer body images). Together, these flows document how Fitflow sits between users, store operations, and external platforms without expanding the diagram into internal processes.

	Each API request is protected by JWT: the middleware reads Authorization:Bearer, calls verifyAccessToken, checks if the user is active, and attaches req.user/permissions.
	3
	Order Processing
	Order Status Automation
	Rental status changes via online service: booking, confirmation, pickup, in rental, return, abort, no-show, etc.; there is an automatic cron job for overdue PendingDeposit and payment status available.
	4
	Rental Management
	Late Return Detection
	Late dates are calculated according to the Vietnamese calendar (UTC+7); compare rentEndDate with the actual payment date.
	5
	Rental Management
	Penalty Calculation
	Late Fee: After the threshold number of days (autoPenaltyLateDays in app.config), apply lateFeeMultiplier daily, update order.lateFee, order.status = 'Late' when conditions are met; when processing item returns, also aggregate damage fee from item/return.
	6
	Rental Management
	Outfit Reservation
	Preventing double booking: Before making a reservation, check if the instance is available within the range [rentStart, rentEnd] (excluding the current booking if necessary); the instance can be moved to Reserved closer to the rental date (HOURS_BEFORE_RESERVED).
	7
	Notification
	Email Notification 
	Send emails via SMTP (Nodemailer): order confirmation, booking, password reset.
	8
	Inventory
	Stock Status Update
	ProductInstance.lifecycleStatus Status: Rented, Available, Reserved, Washing, Repair, Lost, Sold — updated upon booking, return, washing/repair, order cancellation, auto-cancel.
	9
	Reporting
	System Statistics Generator
	API owner analytics: aggregate rental/sales revenue by day-month-year (MongoDB aggregate), rental order statistics, dashboard summary (number of products, staff, customers).

3.2 Quality Attributes
3.2.1 Usability 
User Types: The system caters to both casual users and power users.
   * Casual Users (Customers & Guests): Require no more than 30 minutes of training to navigate, search for gear, and complete purchase or rental transactions.
   * Power Users (Managers & Staff): Training should not exceed 1 hour for efficient use of administrative functions such as product management, shift scheduling, inventory tracking, and reporting.
Task Time:
   * Typical customer actions (e.g., browsing sports gear, checking rental availability, applying vouchers) should take less than 2 minutes.
   * Staff should be able to complete manual inventory entry, shift check-ins, or order status overrides in under 5 minutes.
GUI Standards:
   * Use keyboard shortcuts, hover tooltips, and structural tabs to make the user interface clean and intuitive.
   * Error messages will be descriptive, actionable, and displayed near the source of the problem. Examples: "Please enter a valid email address" or "Password must be at least 8 characters long and contain at least 1 uppercase, 1 lowercase, and 1 numeric character."
   * Elements will be well-aligned, and proper spacing will be maintained to avoid cluttered interfaces, optimizing readability for comprehensive gear specifications.
3.2.2 Performance
   *  Response Time:
   * Customer-facing transactions (e.g., booking, payment) should have an average response time of less than 2 seconds.
   * Administrative tasks (e.g., data entry, report generation) should have an average response time of less than 3 seconds.
   * Throughput: The system must handle 1000 concurrent users and support up to 50 transactions per second during peak times.
   * Capacity: The system must support up to 10,000 active customer accounts and handle up to 500,000 transactions per year.
   * Resource Utilization: The system must use no more than 70% of allocated server CPU and memory resources under peak load.
3.2.3 Reliability
   *  Availability: The system will be available 99.99% of the time, with minimal downtime for maintenance. Scheduled maintenance periods will be during off-peak hours.
   * Mean Time Between Failures (MTBF): 6 months between critical system failures.
   * Mean Time To Repair (MTTR): The system should be recoverable within 30 minutes after a critical failure.
   * Accuracy: All financial transactions must be 100% accurate with real-time updates to customer accounts and bookings.
   *  Defect Rate:
   * Critical Bugs: No more than 1 critical bug per 10,000 lines of code (bugs/KLOC).
   * Significant Bugs: No more than 5 significant bugs per 10,000 lines of code.
   * Minor Bugs: No more than 10 minor bugs per 10,000 lines of code

IV. Software Design Description
1. System Design
1.1 System Architecture 
The architecture of the Fitflow system is designed to ensure modularity, scalability, and maintainability. By applying a layered architectural pattern, the system clearly separates the presentation layer, application layer, and data layer. This separation allows each layer to evolve independently without affecting other components.
The use of RESTful API enables standardized communication between the frontend and backend, ensuring flexibility and compatibility with future extensions such as mobile applications or third-party integrations.
JWT-based authentication enhances system security by providing stateless and secure token-based access control. Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) ensures that system functionalities are restricted according to user roles (Guest, Customer, Staff, Owner).
Simultaneously, the API integrates external services: Cloudinary (images/videos), API4.ai (image processing/clothing fitting), PayOS (payments, with webhook), and SMTP email (notifications).

V. Software Testing Documentation
1. Scope of Testing
The testing phase of the Fitflow – Hoi An Traditional Clothing Sales and Rental Management System project will focus on the thorough verification and validation of the system’s features, functions, and non-functional requirements to ensure that the software meets all specified expectations and is ready for deployment in a production environment. The scope of testing includes the following areas:
1.1. Functional Testing – Core Features and Capabilities
All features and functionalities described in the Software Requirements Specification (SRS) will be included in the testing scope. This ensures that each component of the system performs correctly according to the intended use. Key functional areas to be tested include, but are not limited to:
   * User registration, login, and account management.
   * Search, filter, and retrieve customer information.
   * Customer data operations such as create, update, delete, categorize, and tag.
   * Data analysis, visualization, and report generation based on custom criteria.
   * Workflow handling and request management across different customer interaction stages.
   * Role-based access control and interaction among different user roles (e.g., admin, data analyst, report viewer).
1.2. Non-Functional Testing – System Quality Attributes
In addition to functional verification, the system will undergo rigorous testing of non-functional requirements to evaluate the quality of performance, security, and accessibility. This includes:
   * Performance Testing: Measuring system responsiveness and behavior under various loads, including simultaneous user interactions and large-scale data processing.
   * Security Testing: Verifying that data is securely handled, user authentication and authorization are enforced, and the system is resilient to common vulnerabilities such as SQL injection, cross-site scripting (XSS), etc.
   * Accessibility Testing: Ensuring the system is usable by individuals with disabilities, in compliance with standards like WCAG (Web Content Accessibility Guidelines).
   * Compatibility Testing: Confirming the system operates correctly across various browsers, devices, and operating systems.
   * Stability and Reliability Testing: Assessing the software’s ability to run continuously and correctly over time, especially under prolonged usage or stress conditions.
1.3. Out-of-Scope Components
The following areas will be considered out of scope for the current testing phase due to either incomplete implementation or lower priority at this stage:
   * Features that are planned but not yet implemented in the current version.
   * Enhancements or advanced features reserved for future releases.
   * Non-functional requirements that are not critical to the core functionality or immediate quality of the system, such as:
   * Advanced user experience improvements (e.g., dynamic interface effects or animations).
   * High-level system scalability in distributed environments.
Automated advanced reporting features not required in the initial rollout.
2. Test Strategy
                   In this project, we will apply the Incremental Iterative Methodology to perform software testing.
  

Figure 36. - Incremental Iterative Methodology
 
In the Fitflow project, we will adopt a customized Incremental Iterative Methodology as the foundation of our software testing approach. This methodology aligns with the overall development model of the project, where software components are built progressively through a series of well-defined iterations. The testing process is tightly integrated into each iteration to ensure continuous validation and improvement of the product.
Why Incremental Iterative Methodology?
      * The Incremental Iterative Methodology is selected based on the nature of the Fitflow system, which involves dynamic requirements, evolving user expectations, and the need for regular validation. This methodology allows us to:
      * Develop and test in small, manageable increments: Each iteration includes a set of features that are designed, developed, and tested before moving to the next.
      * Ensure continuous feedback and quality control: With testing embedded into each cycle, the team can detect defects early, assess functionality, and improve the system incrementally.
      * Adapt to changing requirements efficiently: Iterations allow the team to incorporate stakeholder feedback into future cycles, enabling the project to evolve in alignment with user needs.
      * Mitigate risks early: By identifying issues in early stages, this strategy reduces the impact of defects and avoids major rework at later stages of development.
How the Strategy is Applied
Each iteration in the Incremental Iterative Model consists of the following testing phases:
      * Planning and Requirement Review: At the beginning of each iteration, the requirements are reviewed, and test objectives for the current increment are defined. Test cases and scenarios are also prepared based on these objectives.
      * Unit Testing: Developers perform unit tests on individual components as soon as they are developed, ensuring correctness at the code level.
      * Integration Testing: Once modules are ready to be integrated, testers verify the interactions between components, focusing on data flow and interface compatibility.
      * System Testing: The system is tested as a whole to validate end-to-end functionalities, ensuring that both existing and newly added features work together seamlessly.
      * User Acceptance Testing (UAT): Stakeholders are involved at the end of key iterations to verify whether the software meets their needs and expectations.
      * Feedback and Refinement: Results from each testing phase are documented, and feedback is incorporated into the backlog for future iterations or immediate resolution if critical.
Benefits of this Strategy in Fitflow
By applying the Incremental Iterative Test Strategy, Fitflow gains the following advantages:
      * Accelerated delivery of usable software modules in early phases.
      * Early detection and resolution of defects, reducing cost and time.
      * Improved communication and collaboration with stakeholders.
      * Flexibility to adjust the testing scope in response to changing requirements.
      * Greater confidence in the overall system stability and reliability by the time of final delivery.

4. Test Cases
4.1 Unit Testing
Unit Testing is the first and most fundamental level of the software testing process in the development of Hoi An Traditional Clothing Sales and Rental Management System. This phase focuses on validating individual components or modules of the application in isolation to ensure that each unit performs as expected.
Unit testing will encompass both functional testing of internal logic and API testing for internal service endpoints. These tests are primarily conducted by the development team and will be reviewed and approved by the designated team leader. All test results and findings will be systematically documented using Google Sheets for traceability, transparency, and collaboration among team members.
4.1.1 Objectives of Unit Testing
The purpose of unit testing in this project is to:
      * Validate the correctness of individual functions, methods, and components in isolation.
      * Ensure that each code unit adheres to the business logic and functional specifications before integration with other modules.
      * Provide fast feedback to developers when a function or module behaves incorrectly or produces unintended results.
4.1.2 Scope of Unit Testing
Unit testing will cover:
      * Backend components: including API routes, service layers, utility functions, data transformation logic, and validation mechanisms.
      * Frontend components: such as React components, UI logic, state management units, and helper functions.
      * API testing: focusing on the correctness of input/output, response codes, and data consistency at the service level.
Each test case will be constructed to evaluate a specific unit with known input and expected output. Mocking or stubbing will be used as necessary to simulate dependencies and isolate test cases.
4.1.3 Tools and Reporting
      * Testing Tools: Depending on the development environment, tools such as Jest, Mocha, Chai, or Postman may be used to execute unit tests for both front-end and back-end components.
      * Documentation and Tracking: All unit tests will be logged in a centralized Google Sheets document, including:
      * Module/Function Name
      * Test Description
      * Test Input and Expected Output
      * Actual Results
Pass/Fail Status
      * Developer Name and Date
      * Review Status by Team Leader
This record will be regularly updated to reflect the current testing status and will serve as a key reference for quality assurance and audit purposes.
4.1.4 Benefits of Unit Testing
The application of unit testing in this project provides several key benefits:
         * Early Bug Detection: Unit tests allow developers to catch and fix issues at the earliest possible stage, before components are integrated, reducing downstream complications and cost.
         * Improved Code Quality: Writing testable code encourages better software design, resulting in more modular, readable, and maintainable codebases.
         * Faster Refactoring: With a reliable suite of unit tests, developers can confidently refactor code without fear of breaking existing functionality.
         * Acts as Living Documentation: Unit tests serve as real-time, executable documentation of how the system is expected to behave, helping both current and future developers understand the logic behind specific modules.

4.2 Integration Testing
Integration Testing is the second level in the testing process for Hoi An Traditional Clothing Sales and Rental Management System, conducted after individual modules have passed unit testing. This phase is designed to verify that different modules or services work together correctly when integrated. The primary goal is to detect issues that may arise when multiple components interact, such as interface mismatches, data inconsistency, or logical errors during communication between layers.
Integration testing will be performed by QA engineers or developers, depending on the complexity of the integrated components. All results will be documented and reviewed collaboratively to ensure that integrated parts of the application behave as expected under various scenarios.
4.2.1 Objectives of Integration Testing
         * Verify data flow and interaction between modules, components, or services.
         * Ensure compatibility between dependent systems (e.g., front-end to back-end, back-end to database).
         * Detects integration-related defects, such as incorrect input/output mappings, communication breakdowns, or API response mismatches.
4.2.2 Scope of Integration Testing
This testing phase will cover:
         * Integration between front-end and back-end systems, including API endpoints, form submissions, data rendering, and state synchronization.
         * Interaction between internal service layers, such as controllers, services, repositories, and third-party integrations (e.g., payment gateways, external APIs).
         * Middleware and routing logic, including authentication, authorization, and data validation pipelines.
         * Database interaction, focusing on data consistency, CRUD operations, and business logic execution within multi-step workflows.

Test scenarios will simulate realistic workflows that involve multiple components working together, ensuring that the application behaves correctly as a whole.
4.2.3 Tools and Reporting
            * Testing Tools: Tools such as Postman, Jest, Supertest, or Cypress may be used to automate and perform integration testing.
            * Reporting & Documentation: All integration test cases and results will be documented using Google Sheets or a test management tool. Each record will include:
            * Components Involved
            * Test Scenario and Preconditions
            * Expected vs. Actual Results
            * Status (Pass/Fail)
            * Issues Found and Resolutions
            * Test Owner and Review Status
4.2.4 Benefits of Integration Testing
            * Identifies Issues Early in communication and data exchange between modules.
            * Validates Complex Workflows that span multiple components of the application.
            * Reduces Risk before moving into full system testing by ensuring core interactions are reliable.
            * Improves Collaboration between front-end, back-end, and QA teams by clarifying integration expectations.

4.3 System Testing
System Testing is the final and most comprehensive level of functional testing in the development lifecycle of Hoi An Traditional Clothing Sales and Rental Management System. This phase focuses on validating the entire system as a complete and fully integrated product, ensuring that it meets the defined requirements and functions correctly in an environment that closely mirrors the production setup.
System testing will be executed by the Quality Assurance (QA) team, and includes both manual and automated test execution. It is designed to simulate real user behavior, cover end-to-end scenarios, and evaluate whether the application meets both functional and non-functional requirements.
4.3.1 Objectives of System Testing
            * Verify the system’s overall behavior and performance under normal and edge case scenarios.
            * Ensure the application satisfies business requirements as outlined in the specifications.
            * Evaluate the stability and completeness of the product prior to User Acceptance Testing (UAT) or production release.

4.3.2 Scope of System Testing
System testing encompasses a broad range of test categories, including:
               * Functional Testing: Validating user workflows, navigation, data handling, form submission, and business rules.

               * Security Testing: Testing login, authentication, and authorization mechanisms.

               * Error Handling & Recovery: Validating the system’s ability to recover gracefully from invalid input or unexpected conditions.

4.4 Acceptance Testing
User Acceptance Testing (UAT) is the final and most critical phase of the testing process for the Hoi An Traditional Clothing Sales and Rental Management System. This phase focuses on validating the system from the perspective of the end-users (store owners, staff, and customers) to ensure that all features align with real-world business requirements and are ready for official deployment.
4.4.1 Objectives of Acceptance Testing
               * Validate Business Workflows: Ensure the system accurately handles core operations, from account registration and garment management to complex sales and rental transactions.
               * Verify Data Integrity: Confirm that product information, order details, and transaction histories are stored and displayed correctly without data loss.
               * Audit System Responses: Ensure that error messages, success notifications, and automated communications (such as confirmation emails) behave as expected in live scenarios.
               * Final Sign-off: Provide a documented basis for stakeholders to confirm that the software is complete and satisfies the project’s business objectives.
4.4.2 Scope of Acceptance Testing
The UAT scope focuses on high-level business scenarios based on actual operational data:
               * Account Management: Validating registration, login security, and profile management for both customers and administrators.
               * Product & Catalog Management: Verifying the clothing category management, search functionality, and the accuracy of product detail displays.
               * Rent & Buy Workflow: Executing end-to-end tests for adding items to the cart, modifying quantities, applying discount codes, and calculating final totals for both sales and rentals.
               * Payments & Invoicing: Testing integrated payment methods and the accuracy of generated invoices or order summaries.
               * Interaction & Feedback: Confirming the system’s ability to provide immediate feedback after user actions and successfully process customer inquiries or support requests.
4.4.3 Tools and Reporting
               * Testing Environment: Conducted in a Staging environment using sanitized real-world data to mirror the final production site.
               * Documentation and Tracking: All UAT cases are logged in a centralized Google Sheets document for transparency, featuring:
               * Test Case ID: Unique identifiers for each scenario (e.g., AT_01, AT_02).
               * Test Steps: Detailed instructions for the end-user to follow.
               * Expected Results: Desired outcomes based on business rules.
               * Status (Pass/Fail): Real-time recording of the test results.
4.4.4 Benefits of Acceptance Testing
               * Ensures Operational Quality: Catches UI/UX or business logic issues that technical unit or integration tests might have missed.
               * Optimizes Workflow Efficiency: Guarantees that store staff can manage inventory and orders smoothly, reducing the risk of human error during live operations.
               * Builds Stakeholder Confidence: Demonstrating that the system passes real-world scenarios ensures the client that the product is stable and ready for the market.

               * Web Browser: Google Chrome, Microsoft Edge, Firefox
